Sweet Potatoes

Home > Projects > Sweet Potatoes

African Farmers export billions of dollars worth of fresh produce to Europe a year. Naturally competitive in the Horticulture industry, however steep air freight fees, usually around $1kg cut into their profits. If we could figure out a way to utilize lower cost marine freight, around $0.10/kg, we could potentially be increasing farmers income on the order of $0.80 per kg of produce they consume. The trick would be finding a crop that had the shelf life to survive a longer transit time, that was naturally suited to the Gambian climate, and was highly demanded on the global market. Enter the Beauregard Sweet Potato.

If gambian sweet potato farmers could only access the European Market, where Sweet Potatoes commanded a 4x-5x premium, not only would farmers be able to drastically improve their quality of life, but also their capacity to provide for local consumption needs. (Western Sweet Potato Producers such as The United States achieves a yield of around 25tn/ha, whereas Gambian farmers on average realize an average return below 6tn/ha. This differential is largely a result of farmers lacking sufficient capital to invest in the fertilizer and other resources needed to achieve higher yields.

At the same time a push across Africa was developing for the production of more Orange-flesh sweet potatoes. Campaigns such One.Org’s provide much-needed nutrients like vitamins C, A and B6 to undernourished children, helping to avert stunting and ensuring proper growth.f Only 50% of any Sweet Potato crop may not be of premium retail grade,, i.e. too large or too small for retailers, uneconomical to export, but perfect for local consumption. Thus if we could help farmers establish a low cost link to the European markets, the farmers and their families would be able to realize an immediate boost in income, increase their net production of Sweet Potatoes, and increase the adoption of anti-malnutrition super potatoes.

To do this we would have to do two things:

  • Design a Supply Chain capable of delivering Sweet Potatoes to the port of Tilbury for under $0.90/kg
  • Determine which orange fleshed variety demanded by consumers in Europe was most effectively propagated in the Gambia

Phase 1: 2012-2013
  • Decided on Not-For-Profit Model, developed business plan
  • Developed Relationships with potential in-country partners, in particular the Peace Corps and the Brufut
  • Travelled in-country
  • With Partenrs developed strategic plan for action


Phase 2: 2013-2014
  • Entered Do Good Seed Grant competition, awarded maximum grant
  • Developed Suppy Chain Model from farmgate to retail
  • With input from mentors selected Beauregard Sweet Potato variety
  • Procured, shipped and distributed 1500 lbs of seed stock
  • Travelled in-country to manage distribution of seed and initiation of trials
